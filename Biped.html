Wheeled Bipedal Robot

Mechanical Design  ·  Electrical  ·  Firmware

Overview

Grass is a bipedal (two-legged), two-wheeled self-balancing robot with wireless user control that integrates mechanical, electrical, and software design into a single dynamic platform.

The system functions as an inverted pendulum on a cart, maintaining upright stability by actively driving two DC gearmotors mounted at the base of its legs. Because the system is inherently unstable, continuous feedback control is required to prevent tipping and to enable controlled motion.

Balance and motion are achieved using a dual cascaded PID control system — an inner loop that regulates tilt angle and an outer loop that manages translational velocity by generating a tilt setpoint. A custom C# WinForms interface communicates wirelessly via Bluetooth Low Energy for real-time command input and PID tuning.

Details

Mechanical Design

Grass was designed to be printed almost entirely out of PLA with the exception of the tires and the front and back bumpers, keeping costs and print time low. Because the robot would frequently fall during balance testing, the bumpers and battery tabs were printed in TPU for shock absorption and to protect the electronics hidden inside the chassis.

The chassis is split into nine main components and designed around safety, easy access, proper ventilation, and modularity. Since the chassis acts as the point mass of the inverted pendulum, significant care was taken to keep the centre of mass high — at 20 cm above the wheels — while balancing weight evenly front-to-back. This height was selected based on idealised inverted-pendulum calculations and the stall torque of the chosen motors. To keep the system lean, the legs feature weight-saving cut-outs and internal channels to route motor and encoder wires cleanly into the chassis.

Key design decisions Power and signal wires are separated on opposite sides of the chassis to reduce electromagnetic crosstalk. Metal flange connectors replaced plastic shaft collars after repeated stripping under high torque. The final wheel diameter of 10.5 cm balanced backlash reduction against sufficient top speed.

Components & Circuit

The electronics were selected by first calculating the maximum torque needed at a 30° tilt, then working outward from the motor spec to choose every other part. Budget was a hard constraint — the target was under $200 for all components.

Control Theory

The robot is modelled as a wheeled inverted pendulum. Two cascaded PID controllers form the core of the balance and motion system:

Inner loop — Balancing PID Takes the current tilt angle from the IMU and a tilt setpoint from the outer loop. Outputs a desired motor current (saturated at ±4 A). The duty cycle is then calculated from the motor armature equation, accounting for back-EMF, rather than simple open-loop voltage control. Runs at 500 Hz on Core 1 of the ESP32.
Outer loop — Velocity PID Takes a user-commanded velocity setpoint and the current wheel velocity from the encoder. Outputs a tilt angle setpoint for the inner loop. Setting the velocity setpoint to zero makes Grass stand still; non-zero values cause controlled forward or backward motion. Limiting the maximum duty cycle to 128/255 (≈50%) was critical — uncapped PWM caused repeated motor overheating during early testing.

The system was first tuned manually, then a full Simulink model of the inverted pendulum was built to estimate initial PID gains and loop speeds before final hardware tuning. The tilt estimation loop runs at 1 kHz (double the PID rate) to ensure sensor data is never stale when the control loop fires.

Firmware

The firmware runs on the dual-core ESP32 using FreeRTOS. The two cores are deliberately isolated to prevent timing interference:

Core 0 — Sensor & Communication An IMU task reads raw accelerometer and gyroscope data over SPI at 1 kHz, applies scaling factors from the datasheet, and feeds the values into a Madgwick AHRS filter to produce a clean tilt angle estimate. A Bluetooth Low Energy server task handles all incoming command packets from the C# UI, parsing serial byte packets (e.g. 255 Kp Ki Kd) through a state machine to update PID coefficients, velocity setpoints, or trigger an e-stop.
Core 1 — PID & Motor Control A hardware timer fires at 500 Hz via interrupt, releasing a semaphore that wakes the PID task. The task reads encoder counts, computes wheel velocity through a double complementary filter (α = 0.2) to suppress noise spikes, then runs both PID loops using the FastPID integer-math library to minimise per-iteration delay. Motor direction and PWM are written to the DFR0601 controller, with a hard cutoff if tilt exceeds 25° to protect hardware on a fall.

The IMU was initially connected over I²C, but motor noise caused repeated desyncing and sudden data shutdowns. Switching to SPI (more noise-resistant) resolved both issues immediately. The Madgwick filter beta value was tuned to 0.02 (low accelerometer weight) and the onboard LPF cut-off set to 98 Hz, providing a smooth, low-latency tilt estimate that tracks slow tilts without reacting to vibration spikes.

Wireless User Interface

A custom C# WinForms application on a Windows PC acts as the BLE client. It scans for nearby BLE devices, pairs to the ESP32 ("Grass"), and presents a virtual controller with directional buttons, a variable-speed slider (−10 to +10 RPM), real-time PID tuning fields, a tilt-offset adjust, a sync button, and a large red E-Stop.

Commands are serialised as plain UTF-8 strings with a leading command byte (e.g. 251 5 0 for slow forward, 123 234 69 for e-stop). BLE range exceeded the full length of the instrumentation lab with no measurable latency. Attempting to add a live telemetry chart overwhelmed the BLE stack and caused dangerous command lag, so it was removed in favour of stability.

Reflection

✦

What Went Well

The electronics and circuit design worked on the first try with no major iterations. Every component chosen met its rated specification. The mechanical chassis went through just one significant revision, and the BLE range far exceeded expectations — Grass maintained a stable connection from one end of the instrumentation lab to the other. Most importantly, the robot achieved all of its primary goals: self-balancing for over five minutes, forward and backward movement, impulse recovery, and wireless control.

✦

Challenges

The motors were the largest source of problems. Two motors failed during testing — one overheated due to repeated high-speed direction reversals, and another had a brush burn out — causing significant delays waiting for replacements. Brushed DC gearmotors also introduced persistent backlash that the controller could not fully compensate for, resulting in small oscillations around the balance point. On the firmware side, I²C noise corruption from the motors required a full switch to SPI, and getting multiprocessing right on the ESP32 — separating time-critical PID execution from sensing and BLE without priority inversions — took considerable experimentation. Trying to tune the PIDs by hand without a simulation cost several days of wasted effort before we built a Simulink model.

✦

What I Would Do Differently

Switch to brushless DC motors from the start. BLDC motors eliminate brush wear, reduce backlash, and provide more symmetric response between the two wheels. I would also start with a Simulink model before touching hardware for any future control-theory project — the simulation that saved us in the final week should have been the first step. On the firmware side, a more powerful MCU with additional cores would allow wireless data streaming without sacrificing control loop speed, removing the need for serial print workarounds during tuning. Finally, I'd design a PCB rather than using off-the-shelf modules to clean up wiring and reduce the chassis size.
